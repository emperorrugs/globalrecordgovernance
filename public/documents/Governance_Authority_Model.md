## Authority Separation

GRGF explicitly distinguishes between:

- **Institutional authority** — the formal mandate of an organization
- **Individual actors** — persons exercising delegated authority
- **Delegated mandates** — specific scopes of authority granted to individuals or units

Authority is treated as a recordable attribute, not an assumption. This prevents informal power misuse, post-hoc justification, and ambiguity in accountability.

## Decision–Action–Omission Separation

GRGF formally separates:

| Category | Definition | Governance Implication |
|----------|------------|----------------------|
| **Decision** | Intent expressed by an authority | Creates expectation of action |
| **Action** | Execution of a decision or mandate | Fulfills or violates obligation |
| **Omission** | Failure to decide or act when required | May constitute breach of duty |

Each is recorded differently, because each carries different governance implications.

## Accountability Framework

GRGF enables accountability by preserving:

- **Chain of authority** — who delegated what to whom
- **Temporal responsibility** — when obligations arose and when they were (or were not) met
- **Jurisdictional scope** — what territory and subject matter were covered

### What oversight bodies may do:
- Verify records
- Confirm absence of records
- Audit authority chains

### What they may not do:
- Alter records
- Suppress records
- Rewrite governance history

**Stewardship is separated from control by design.**

## Anti-Capture Mechanisms

Five structural safeguards prevent institutional capture:

| Code | Mechanism | Purpose |
|------|-----------|---------|
| AC-01 | Governance independence | No single entity controls the framework |
| AC-02 | Transparency requirements | All governance actions are recorded |
| AC-03 | Appeals mechanism | Structured challenge and review process |
| AC-04 | Rotation requirements | Leadership term limits and succession |
| AC-05 | External audit mandate | Regular independent assessment |
