## The Omission Problem

In governance, omissions can be as consequential as actions. A failure to act — a missed deadline, an uninvestigated complaint, an unenforced regulation — can cause harm equal to or greater than a wrongful decision.

Yet most governance systems only record what *was* done. They are structurally blind to what *was not* done.

## GRGF's Approach

GRGF formally recognizes and records:

- **Recorded actions** — what institutions did
- **Recorded decisions** — what institutions decided
- **Recorded non-decisions** — what institutions failed to decide when required
- **Recorded delays** — when institutional timelines were breached
- **Recorded absences of authority** — when no mandate existed for claimed actions

This distinction is essential for accountability, oversight, and judicial review.

## Why This Matters

### For Institutions
GRGF strengthens institutions by:
- Protecting honest actors with verifiable proof of diligent action
- Clarifying responsibility chains across administrations
- Reducing ambiguity in mandate and delegation
- Preserving institutional memory across political transitions

### For Democracy
GRGF supports:
- Rule of law through provable governance
- Administrative continuity across elections
- Public trust through verifiable accountability
- Anti-corruption through transparent record-keeping

### For Oversight
Auditors, courts, and oversight bodies gain:
- Independent verification of institutional actions
- Proof of omission when obligations were unmet
- Non-repudiable timelines for accountability
- Cross-institutional traceability

## Design Guarantee

**GRGF does not politicize governance records.** It preserves facts without interpretation, enabling accountability mechanisms to function without political distortion.
